Organic Chemistry

Part 1 — Introduction and Carbon's Uniqueness

Why Carbon?

Organic chemistry is the chemistry of carbon-containing compounds. There are over 10 million known organic compounds — more than all other compounds combined. The reason is carbon's extraordinary versatility.

What Makes Carbon Special

4 valence electrons: Carbon can form exactly 4 covalent bonds — in any combination of single, double, or triple bonds.

Catenation: Carbon bonds strongly to other carbon atoms, forming chains of any length — from 2 carbons (ethane) to thousands (polymers, DNA).

Multiple bond types: Carbon forms stable single, double, and triple bonds with itself and with H, O, N, S, and halogens.

Ring structures: Carbon chains can close on themselves to form cyclic compounds — from cyclopentane (5C ring) to benzene (6C aromatic ring).

Functional Groups

The carbon chain is just a skeleton. What determines a molecule's chemical behaviour is its functional group — a specific atom or arrangement of atoms that reacts in a characteristic way, regardless of how long the carbon chain is.

Alkane (no functional group): C–C single bonds only. CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Relatively unreactive.

Alkene (C=C): Double bond. CₙH₂ₙ. Addition reactions. Reactive.

Alcohol (–OH): Hydroxyl group. Combustion, oxidation, dehydration.

Carboxylic acid (–COOH): Acidic. Forms esters with alcohols.

Ester (–COO–): Sweet smelling. Formed from acid + alcohol.

Amine (–NH₂): Basic. Related to ammonia.

Halogenoalkane (–X): C bonded to halogen. Undergoes substitution.

IUPAC Nomenclature — Naming Organic Compounds

Organic compounds need systematic names so that every chemist knows exactly what molecule is being referred to. IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) rules give us a universal system.

Step 1: Find the longest carbon chain — this gives the root name.

1C = meth-, 2C = eth-, 3C = prop-, 4C = but-, 5C = pent-, 6C = hex-, 7C = hept-, 8C = oct-

Step 2: Identify the functional group — this gives the suffix.

Alkane = -ane, Alkene = -ene, Alcohol = -ol, Aldehyde = -al, Ketone = -one, Carboxylic acid = -oic acid

Step 3: Number the chain from the end that gives the functional group the lowest number.

Step 4: Name substituents (branches, halogens) as prefixes with their position number.

Example: Naming Practice

CH₃CH₂CH₂OH: 3-carbon chain (prop-), alcohol (-ol), OH on carbon 1 → propan-1-ol

CH₃CHCH₂CH₃ with Cl on C2: 4-carbon chain (but-), chloro on C2 → 2-chlorobutane

CH₃CH=CHCH₃: 4-carbon chain, double bond between C2 and C3 → but-2-ene

IUPAC names are unambiguous — any chemist in any country can draw exactly the right molecule from the name. Always number to give the key feature the lowest locant.

Homologous Series

A homologous series is a family of compounds that share the same functional group and general formula, differing by CH₂ each time. Members show gradually changing physical properties (boiling point, solubility) but similar chemical properties.

Alkanes: CH₄, C₂H₆, C₃H₈, C₄H₁₀... each differs by −CH₂−

Boiling point increases with chain length (more surface area → stronger intermolecular forces).

All alkanes undergo combustion — but react slightly differently depending on chain length.
