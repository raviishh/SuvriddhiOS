States of Matter

Matter and Its Nature — Part 1

What Actually Is a "State"?

When we say matter exists in different "states," we're really describing how the particles inside it are arranged and how much freedom they have to move. The chemical identity of the substance doesn't change at all — water is H₂O whether it's ice, liquid, or steam. What changes is the amount of energy the particles have and how strongly they're attracted to each other.

There are three common states: solid, liquid, and gas. A fourth state — plasma — exists at extreme temperatures (like in stars), but we won't go there for now.

Solids

In a solid, particles are packed tightly together in a regular arrangement — a lattice. They're held in place by strong forces of attraction and can only vibrate about fixed positions. They can't move past each other.

Fixed shape ✓ — particles locked in place

Fixed volume ✓ — particles can't be pushed together (nearly incompressible)

Cannot flow ✓ — no freedom to move past each other

High density — particles packed tightly

The vibration of particles in a solid increases with temperature. Heat up a solid enough, and eventually those vibrations overcome the forces holding particles in their fixed positions — the solid melts.

Liquids

In a liquid, particles are still close together (similar density to a solid) but the forces between them are weaker. Particles can slide past each other, which is why liquids flow. They're not free to move around completely — they still attract each other — but they have enough energy to change positions.

No fixed shape ✓ — takes the shape of its container

Fixed volume ✓ — particles still close together, barely compressible

Can flow ✓ — particles slide past each other

High density (similar to solid)

Gases

In a gas, particles have so much energy they've completely broken free from each other. They move rapidly in all directions, filling whatever container they're in. The average distance between gas particles is roughly 10× larger than in a liquid — mostly empty space.

No fixed shape ✓ — fills container

No fixed volume ✓ — expands to fill any space

Compressible ✓ — lots of empty space between particles

Low density — particles far apart

State Changes

Heating gives particles more kinetic energy. At certain temperatures, enough energy has been added that particles can overcome the forces holding them in their current state — a state change occurs.

State changes are physical changes — no new substance is formed. The chemical formula stays identical. Ice, water, and steam are all H₂O. This means state changes are always reversible.

Example: Heating Curve for Water

As you heat ice from −20°C to 120°C, temperature rises steadily — but then flatlines at 0°C and again at 100°C.

At 0°C (melting point): all added energy goes into breaking the bonds holding the ice lattice together, not into raising temperature. Temperature stays flat until all the ice has melted.

At 100°C (boiling point): all energy goes into separating liquid water particles into gas.

The flat sections on a heating curve are where state changes happen. During a state change, temperature doesn't rise even though heat is being added.

Diffusion

Diffusion is the gradual mixing of particles due to random motion. It happens in liquids and gases — not solids (particles can't move). The rate of diffusion is faster in gases (particles move more freely) and increases with temperature.

A drop of ink in water gradually spreads throughout the glass — that's diffusion. Opening a perfume bottle causes scent molecules to gradually spread through the room. Both happen because particles are in constant random motion and naturally mix over time.
