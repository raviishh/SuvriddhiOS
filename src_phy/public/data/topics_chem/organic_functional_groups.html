Organic Chemistry

Part 4 — Carboxylic Acids, Esters, and Amines

Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids contain the –COOH (carboxyl) functional group — a carbonyl (C=O) and a hydroxyl (–OH) on the same carbon. The O–H bond is polar and the H is acidic, ionizing partially in water.

General formula: CₙH₂ₙO₂ (for straight-chain acids)

Methanoic acid: HCOOH (formic acid — in ant stings)

Ethanoic acid: CH₃COOH (acetic acid — in vinegar, pKₐ = 4.76)

Propanoic acid: C₂H₅COOH

Butanoic acid: C₃H₇COOH (smells of rancid butter)

Properties of Carboxylic Acids

Weak acids — partially ionize in water. Lower pH than expected for their concentration vs strong acids.

Form hydrogen bonds — higher boiling points than alcohols of similar size.

React with bases → carboxylate salt + water: CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O

React with carbonates → salt + CO₂ + water: 2CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂

React with alcohols → ester + water (esterification)

Esters

Esters are formed when a carboxylic acid reacts with an alcohol in a condensation reaction (water is eliminated). They have the functional group –COO– and are characterised by sweet, fruity smells.

Esterification:

Carboxylic acid + Alcohol ⇌ Ester + Water

CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O

(ethanoic acid + ethanol → ethyl ethanoate + water)

Conditions: concentrated H₂SO₄ catalyst, heat. Reversible — remove water to shift equilibrium right.

Naming esters: [alkyl from alcohol]-yl [acid minus -ic + -ate]

CH₃COOC₂H₅: ethyl ethanoate (pear drop smell)

HCOOC₅H₁₁: pentyl methanoate (peppermint)

CH₃COOC₅H₁₁: pentyl ethanoate (banana)

Hydrolysis: Esters can be broken back down with water (acid or base catalyst). Base hydrolysis (saponification) is used in soap-making — fats (esters) + NaOH → glycerol + fatty acid salts (soap).

Amines

Amines contain the –NH₂ group (or –NHR, –NR₂). They are derivatives of ammonia (NH₃), with one or more H replaced by carbon groups. Amines are basic — the nitrogen lone pair accepts protons.

Primary amine (–NH₂): One C attached to N. Methylamine: CH₃NH₂

Secondary amine (–NH–): Two C groups on N. Dimethylamine: (CH₃)₂NH

Tertiary amine (–N–): Three C groups on N. Trimethylamine: (CH₃)₃N

Many biologically important molecules contain amine groups: amino acids (proteins), DNA bases, neurotransmitters (dopamine, serotonin), and many drugs.

Amines as Bases

CH₃NH₂ + H₂O ⇌ CH₃NH₃⁺ + OH⁻

The nitrogen lone pair accepts a proton from water, producing OH⁻ → solution is basic.

CH₃NH₂ + HCl → CH₃NH₃⁺Cl⁻ (methylammonium chloride salt)

Amines smell characteristically "fishy" — trimethylamine is responsible for the smell of rotting fish. Cooking with lemon juice (acid) neutralizes these amines and reduces the smell.

Isomerism — Structure vs Formula

Isomers have the same molecular formula but different structures — and therefore different properties.

Structural isomers: Different connectivity. C₄H₁₀ → n-butane and methylpropane.

Positional isomers: Same functional group, different position. Propan-1-ol vs propan-2-ol (both C₃H₇OH).

Functional group isomers: Same formula, different functional group. C₂H₆O can be ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) or methoxymethane (CH₃OCH₃ — an ether).

Stereoisomers (E/Z): Same connectivity but different spatial arrangement around a C=C double bond. But-2-ene has E (trans) and Z (cis) forms with different boiling points.
