Chemical Kinetics

Part 2 — Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

Four Ways to Change the Rate

Every factor that affects rate does so by changing either: (a) the frequency of collisions, or (b) the proportion of those collisions that are effective (have E ≥ Eₐ and correct orientation). Keep this framework in mind — it explains all four factors.

Factor 1: Temperature

Increasing temperature gives particles more kinetic energy. Two effects:

• Particles move faster → more frequent collisions

• More particles have energy ≥ Eₐ → higher proportion of effective collisions

The second effect dominates. A 10°C rise roughly doubles the rate of many reactions because the fraction of particles exceeding Eₐ increases dramatically (exponential relationship — Arrhenius equation).

On the Maxwell-Boltzmann curve: curve flattens and shifts right. Area to the right of Eₐ increases significantly.

Factor 2: Concentration (or Pressure for Gases)

More particles in the same volume → more frequent collisions per unit time → higher rate.

The proportion of effective collisions doesn't change (Eₐ is unchanged), only the frequency.

For gases: increasing pressure at constant temperature = more particles per unit volume = same effect as increased concentration.

Halving the concentration roughly halves the rate (for a first-order reaction). The exact relationship depends on the rate law for that specific reaction.

Factor 3: Surface Area

Reactions between a solid and a liquid/gas only happen at the solid's surface. Interior particles are inaccessible.

Smaller particle size → more surface exposed → more collision sites → more frequent effective collisions → faster rate.

Powder vs lump: a 1 cm cube has 6 cm² surface. Divide into 1mm cubes: 6000 mm² = 60 cm² surface — 10× more.

Total amount of reaction (yield) is unchanged — just how fast it happens.

Factor 4: Catalysts

A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy. More particles have energy ≥ the new (lower) Eₐ → more effective collisions → faster rate.

Crucially: the catalyst is not consumed. It participates in the reaction but is regenerated. It doesn't change the overall energy change of the reaction (ΔH) — only how fast equilibrium is reached.

On Maxwell-Boltzmann curve: Eₐ line shifts left. The area to the right of the new Eₐ is much larger.

Types of catalysts:

Homogeneous: Same phase as reactants. H₂SO₄ in esterification, enzyme in fermentation.

Heterogeneous: Different phase. Ni in hydrogenation (solid catalyst, gas reactants), Fe in Haber process, Pt in catalytic converters.

Enzymes are biological catalysts — protein molecules with highly specific active sites. They're extraordinarily efficient but work only in narrow temperature/pH ranges. Above ~40°C, enzymes denature (unfold) and lose activity.

Example: Catalytic Converter in Cars

Contains Pt, Pd, Rh catalysts on a honeycomb structure (huge surface area).

Converts toxic exhaust gases:

2CO + 2NO → 2CO₂ + N₂    (CO and NO → harmless CO₂ and N₂)

2C₈H₁₈ + 25O₂ → 16CO₂ + 18H₂O    (unburned fuel oxidized)

The honeycomb design maximizes surface area — the heterogeneous catalyst must contact reactant gases. Platinum works but is extremely expensive; research into cheaper alternatives is ongoing.
