Organic Chemistry

Part 2 — Alkanes

Saturated Hydrocarbons

Alkanes are the simplest organic compounds — chains of carbon atoms bonded only by single C–C bonds, with the remaining valences filled by hydrogen. "Saturated" means every carbon is bonded to the maximum possible number of hydrogens — no room to add more without breaking a bond.

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂

Methane: CH₄    Ethane: C₂H₆    Propane: C₃H₈    Butane: C₄H₁₀

Pentane: C₅H₁₂    Hexane: C₆H₁₄    Heptane: C₇H₁₆    Octane: C₈H₁₈

Physical Properties — Trends in the Series

Boiling point increases with chain length. Longer chains have more surface area, meaning stronger London dispersion forces (van der Waals) between molecules. More energy needed to separate them.

CH₄: −161°C    C₄H₁₀: −1°C    C₈H₁₈ (octane): 126°C

State at room temperature: C₁–C₄ are gases; C₅–C₁₇ are liquids; C₁₈+ are waxy solids.

Viscosity increases with chain length — longer chains tangle together more.

All alkanes are immiscible with water — non-polar molecules don't mix with polar water.

Combustion — The Main Reaction

Alkanes are relatively unreactive — the C–H and C–C single bonds are strong and non-polar. Their main reaction is combustion with oxygen, releasing large amounts of energy. This is why they're used as fuels.

Complete Combustion

Excess oxygen: all carbon → CO₂, all hydrogen → H₂O

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

General: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ + (3n+1)/2 O₂ → nCO₂ + (n+1)H₂O

Incomplete Combustion

Limited oxygen: produces CO (toxic) and/or C (soot) instead of CO₂.

2CH₄ + 3O₂ → 2CO + 4H₂O    (CO produced)

CH₄ + O₂ → C + 2H₂O    (soot produced)

CO binds to haemoglobin ~200× more strongly than O₂ — even small concentrations are lethal. Carbon monoxide poisoning kills because red blood cells can no longer carry oxygen.

Isomerism in Alkanes

For C₄ and above, alkanes can have different structural arrangements — structural isomers — same molecular formula but different connectivity.

Isomers of C₄H₁₀

n-butane: CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃ (straight chain)   BP: −1°C

Methylpropane: (CH₃)₃CH (branched)   BP: −12°C

Branched isomers have lower boiling points — more compact shape, less surface area contact between molecules, weaker intermolecular forces.

C₅H₁₂ has 3 isomers. C₆H₁₄ has 5. C₁₀H₂₂ has 75. The number of possible isomers grows rapidly with chain length — another reason organic chemistry has millions of compounds.

Substitution reactions: Alkanes react with halogens (Cl₂, Br₂) under UV light in a free radical chain reaction. H atoms are replaced one by one: CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl. This is called free radical substitution — it's not selective and produces a mixture of products.
