Chemical Bonding

Part 1 — Ionic Bonding

Why Do Atoms Bond At All?

Isolated atoms are generally in a higher energy state than bonded atoms. Bonding releases energy and produces a more stable arrangement. The driving force behind all chemical bonding is the same: atoms rearranging their electrons to reach a lower energy, more stable configuration — usually resembling the nearest noble gas.

The Octet Rule

Most atoms bond in ways that give them 8 electrons in their outermost shell — a full, stable octet. Hydrogen and helium are exceptions (stable with 2). This rule explains the vast majority of bonding for Period 2 and 3 elements.

Exceptions exist (e.g. PCl₅ with 10 electrons around P, BF₃ with only 6 around B), but for IB SL the octet rule holds in most cases you'll encounter.

What Is Ionic Bonding?

Ionic bonding occurs when one atom transfers one or more electrons to another atom. This happens between atoms with very different electronegativities — typically a metal and a non-metal. The metal loses electrons to become a positive ion (cation); the non-metal gains them to become a negative ion (anion). The resulting opposite charges attract each other strongly.

Na → Na⁺ + e⁻    (oxidation — loses electron)

Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻     (reduction — gains electron)

Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl   (ionic bond — electrostatic attraction)

The Ionic Lattice

Ionic compounds don't exist as individual pairs of ions. They form giant 3D structures called ionic lattices, where each ion is surrounded by several ions of opposite charge. In NaCl, each Na⁺ is surrounded by 6 Cl⁻ ions, and each Cl⁻ by 6 Na⁺ — a 6:6 coordination.

Properties of Ionic Compounds

High melting and boiling points: The ionic lattice is held together by strong electrostatic forces acting in all directions. A lot of energy is needed to overcome them. NaCl melts at 801°C.

Hard but brittle: The lattice is rigid. When struck, layers shift — ions of like charge align and repel, shattering the crystal.

Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved: In the solid state, ions are locked in place. When melted or dissolved, ions are free to move and carry charge. Solid ionic compounds do NOT conduct.

Often soluble in water: Water molecules (polar) are attracted to the ions and pull them away from the lattice — dissolving it.

Example: Magnesium Oxide (MgO)

Mg is in Group 2 → loses 2 electrons → Mg²⁺

O is in Group 16 → gains 2 electrons → O²⁻

Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ have charges of ±2, so the attraction is twice as strong as Na⁺/Cl⁻.

MgO melting point: 2852°C — far higher than NaCl (801°C).

Higher ion charges → stronger lattice forces → higher melting point. This is why MgO is used in furnace linings.

Working out ionic formula: The formula must be electrically neutral overall. Al³⁺ and O²⁻: need 2 Al³⁺ and 3 O²⁻ to balance (total charge 6+ and 6−). Formula = Al₂O₃.
