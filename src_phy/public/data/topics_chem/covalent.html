Chemical Bonding

Part 2 — Covalent Bonding

Sharing Instead of Transferring

When two non-metals bond, neither one has a strong enough pull to completely steal electrons from the other. Instead, they share electron pairs. Each atom contributes one electron to a shared pair, and both atoms count those shared electrons as their own. This is a covalent bond.

Covalent Bond

A covalent bond is a shared pair of electrons between two atoms. The bond forms because both nuclei attract the shared electrons, holding the atoms together. The more pairs shared, the stronger and shorter the bond.

Single bond: 1 shared pair (e.g. H–H, H–Cl)

Double bond: 2 shared pairs (e.g. O=O, C=O)

Triple bond: 3 shared pairs (e.g. N≡N)

Lewis Dot Structures

Lewis (dot-and-cross) diagrams show the valence electrons of each atom and how they're shared or kept as lone pairs. Each line in a structural formula represents one shared pair (one bond).

Polar vs Non-Polar Covalent Bonds

Not all covalent bonds are equal. When two atoms of different electronegativity share electrons, the electrons are pulled more towards the more electronegative atom. This creates an unequal bond — a polar covalent bond.

Non-polar covalent: Electrons shared equally. Same element (H–H, Cl–Cl) or similar electronegativity. No partial charges.

Polar covalent: Electrons shared unequally. δ⁺ on the less electronegative atom, δ⁻ on the more electronegative atom.

Example: H–Cl. Cl is more electronegative than H → electrons pulled towards Cl → Cl is δ⁻, H is δ⁺.

If EN difference is large enough (> ~1.8 on Pauling scale): effectively ionic — electron essentially transferred.

Properties of Covalent Compounds

Simple molecular compounds (H₂O, CO₂, HCl):

Low melting and boiling points — the covalent bonds within molecules are strong, but the forces between molecules (intermolecular forces) are weak. Little energy needed to separate the molecules.

Usually poor conductors — no free ions or electrons (except when ionized in water).

Can be polar or non-polar — affects solubility ("like dissolves like").

Giant covalent structures (diamond, SiO₂):

Every atom is covalently bonded to neighbours in a giant 3D network. Extremely high melting points. Very hard. Don't conduct (usually).

Example: Why Does Ice Float?

Water molecules are polar (O is δ⁻, each H is δ⁺). In ice, molecules form hydrogen bonds in a hexagonal lattice that spaces them further apart than in liquid water — making ice less dense than liquid water. Ice floats.

This is a direct consequence of polar covalent bonds creating partial charges, which then create hydrogen bonds between molecules.

From bond type → polarity → intermolecular forces → physical property. The chain of reasoning always runs in this direction.
