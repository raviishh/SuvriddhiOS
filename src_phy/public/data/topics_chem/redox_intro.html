Redox Reactions

Part 1 — Oxidation, Reduction, and Electron Transfer

What Is a Redox Reaction?

Redox reactions involve the transfer of electrons from one species to another. They are everywhere: the batteries powering your devices, the rusting of iron, cellular respiration, photosynthesis, and the extraction of metals from their ores. Understanding redox is understanding how energy flows at the atomic level.

OIL RIG — The Rule You'll Never Forget

Oxidation Is Loss    Reduction Is Gain

(of electrons)

Oxidation and reduction always occur together. You cannot have one without the other — if one species loses electrons, another must gain them. A reaction involving both is called a redox reaction.

Oxidation: Loss of electrons. The species losing electrons is oxidized.

Reduction: Gain of electrons. The species gaining electrons is reduced.

Oxidizing agent: The species that causes oxidation in something else. It gains electrons itself — so it is reduced.

Reducing agent: The species that causes reduction in something else. It loses electrons itself — so it is oxidized.

"The oxidizing agent gets reduced. The reducing agent gets oxidized." — easy to confuse, but makes logical sense.

Half-Equations

Any redox reaction can be split into two half-equations — one for the oxidation, one for the reduction. This makes it easier to see exactly what's happening to electrons.

Oxidation half-equation: Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻

Reduction half-equation: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)

Adding the two half-equations (and cancelling the 2e⁻ on both sides):

Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s)   ← overall ionic equation

Balancing half-equations:

1. Balance atoms (not H and O first).

2. Balance O by adding H₂O.

3. Balance H by adding H⁺.

4. Balance charge by adding e⁻.

Then multiply each half-equation so the electrons cancel when added together.

Example: MnO₄⁻ + Fe²⁺ in Acid Solution

Reduction: MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O

Oxidation: Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ + e⁻   (×5 to match electrons)

Combined: MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5Fe²⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O + 5Fe³⁺

MnO₄⁻ is the oxidizing agent (gets reduced from Mn⁷⁺ to Mn²⁺).

Fe²⁺ is the reducing agent (gets oxidized to Fe³⁺).

Potassium permanganate (KMnO₄) is one of the most powerful and commonly used oxidizing agents in chemistry — its deep purple colour disappearing as Mn⁷⁺ becomes colourless Mn²⁺ is used as its own indicator in titrations.
