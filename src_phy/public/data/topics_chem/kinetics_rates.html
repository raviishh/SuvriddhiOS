Chemical Kinetics

Part 1 — Collision Theory and Reaction Rates

How Fast Is Fast?

Chemical reactions happen at wildly different speeds. Explosions are complete in microseconds. Rusting takes years. Radioactive decay of some isotopes takes billions of years. Chemical kinetics is the study of why, and what we can do about it.

Rate of reaction: The change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time.

Rate = Δ[concentration] / Δtime    units: mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹

Rate decreases over time as reactants are consumed (unless it's a zero-order reaction).

Collision Theory

For a chemical reaction to occur, reactant particles must collide. But not just any collision will do — two conditions must be met simultaneously:

Conditions for an Effective Collision

1. Sufficient energy: The combined kinetic energy of the colliding particles must be ≥ the activation energy (Eₐ). If the collision is too gentle, particles just bounce off.

2. Correct orientation: The reactive parts of the molecules must be facing each other at the moment of collision. A collision with the wrong geometry won't produce a reaction even if the energy is sufficient.

Activation Energy and Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

Not all particles in a sample have the same energy — they have a distribution of energies. The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution shows how many particles have each energy level at a given temperature.

Key features of the Maxwell-Boltzmann curve:

• Starts at origin (no particles with zero energy)

• Rises to a peak (most probable energy), then falls asymptotically

• Never touches the x-axis on the right (small fraction always has very high energy)

• The area under the curve to the right of Eₐ = fraction of particles that can react

• At higher temperature: curve flattens and shifts right — more particles exceed Eₐ

Measuring Rate of Reaction

Gas produced: Collect in a syringe and measure volume over time. CaCO₃ + HCl → CO₂ collected.

Mass lost: Put on balance, record mass as gas escapes. CaCO₃ + HCl, mass decreases as CO₂ is released.

Colour change: Use colorimetry or visual observation. Na₂S₂O₃ + HCl produces sulfur — solution goes cloudy. Time until cross underneath is obscured (clock reaction).

pH change: Monitor with pH meter for reactions involving H⁺ or OH⁻.

Conductance: If ionic concentration changes, electrical conductivity changes.

Example: The Thiosulfate Clock Reaction

Na₂S₂O₃(aq) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + SO₂(g) + S(s) + H₂O(l)

Sulfur forms a white precipitate that makes the solution cloudy. A cross on paper under the flask disappears when enough S has formed.

Measure: time for cross to disappear. Rate ∝ 1/time.

Vary: concentration of Na₂S₂O₃ or temperature to investigate effects on rate.

1/time is proportional to rate only if you're comparing when the same amount of product has formed each time — which this experiment does. It's a classic IB practical.
