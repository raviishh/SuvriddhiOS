Organic Chemistry

Carboxylic Acids

The –COOH Group

Carboxylic acids contain the carboxyl group (–COOH) — a carbon double-bonded to an oxygen and single-bonded to an –OH group. This combination makes them acidic: the O–H bond is weakened by the electron-withdrawing effect of the adjacent C=O, so the hydrogen is released relatively easily as H⁺.

General Formula

CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH   or written as   CₙH₂ₙO₂   (for straight-chain saturated carboxylic acids)

The –COOH carbon counts as C1 when naming.

Acidity of Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids are weak acids — they partially ionize in water, establishing an equilibrium:

CH₃COOH(aq) ⇌ CH₃COO⁻(aq) + H⁺(aq)

The CH₃COO⁻ ion formed is the ethanoate ion (acetate) — the conjugate base of ethanoic acid.

Only about 1% of ethanoic acid molecules are ionized at room temperature in a typical solution — much less than strong acids like HCl (≈100% ionized).

Why are carboxylic acids more acidic than alcohols? Both have an –OH group, but in a carboxylic acid the adjacent C=O withdraws electron density from the O–H bond, making it easier to release H⁺. The resulting carboxylate anion (–COO⁻) is also stabilized by resonance — the negative charge is delocalized across both oxygens.

Reactions of Carboxylic Acids

1. Reaction with bases (neutralization):

CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O

Produces a carboxylate salt. Sodium ethanoate (CH₃COONa) is used as a food preservative.

2. Reaction with carbonates:

2CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂

CO₂ gas produced — effervescence is observed. Test: CO₂ turns limewater milky.

3. Reaction with reactive metals:

2CH₃COOH + Mg → (CH₃COO)₂Mg + H₂

Like other acids — hydrogen gas produced, metal dissolves.

4. Esterification (with alcohols):

CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O

Produces an ester — covered in the next lesson. Requires concentrated H₂SO₄ catalyst and heat. Reversible reaction.

Physical Properties and Trends

Boiling points: Higher than alkanes or alcohols of similar molecular mass. Carboxylic acids form hydrogen-bonded dimers — pairs of molecules linked by two H-bonds simultaneously — which takes extra energy to break.

Solubility: Short-chain acids (C1–C4) are fully miscible with water. As chain length increases, the non-polar carbon chain dominates and solubility drops. Long-chain carboxylic acids (fatty acids like stearic acid, C₁₈) are practically insoluble in water.

Smell: Short-chain acids have sharp, unpleasant smells (vinegar, rancid butter). Longer-chain acids are almost odourless.

Example: Comparing Acidity

Rank by increasing acidity: ethanol, ethanoic acid, hydrochloric acid.

Ethanol (C₂H₅OH): Not an acid in practice — the –OH barely releases H⁺ in water. Effectively neutral.

Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH): Weak acid. About 1% ionized. pH ≈ 2.9 for 0.1 mol/dm³.

HCl: Strong acid. ~100% ionized. pH ≈ 1 for 0.1 mol/dm³.

Order of increasing acidity: ethanol < ethanoic acid < HCl

The –COOH group is unique: the C=O withdraws electrons and the resulting anion is resonance-stabilized, making carboxylic acids far more acidic than alcohols despite both containing –OH.
