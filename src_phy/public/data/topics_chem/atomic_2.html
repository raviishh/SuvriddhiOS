Atomic Structure

Part 2 — Electron Shells and Configuration

Where Are the Electrons?

Electrons don't orbit the nucleus like planets around a sun — that's the old Bohr model, which is a useful simplification but not the full picture. What we do know is that electrons occupy specific energy levels (also called shells or principal quantum levels). Each level has a maximum capacity, and electrons fill from the lowest energy level outward.

For the elements in the first three periods (the ones you need at IB SL/HL), the shell model works well enough:

Shell 1: Maximum 2 electrons

Shell 2: Maximum 8 electrons

Shell 3: Maximum 8 electrons (for elements Z ≤ 20)

Shell 4: Maximum 18 electrons (though for IB purposes: 8 first, then fills further)

Writing Electron Configurations

Electron configuration is written as a series of numbers showing how many electrons are in each shell, starting from the innermost. For IB Chemistry (SL), the simple shell notation is sufficient.

Format: Shell 1, Shell 2, Shell 3, ...

Fill each shell to its maximum before moving to the next.

Noble gas configurations: He = 2, Ne = 2,8, Ar = 2,8,8. These are the "stable" arrangements. When other atoms bond, they're trying to reach one of these configurations.

Valence Electrons and the Periodic Table

The number of electrons in the outermost shell (valence electrons) is what determines chemical behavior — and it directly corresponds to the group number.

Example: Predicting Configuration from Position

Element X is in Group 16, Period 3.

Period 3 → 3 shells. Group 16 → 6 valence electrons.

Configuration: 2, 8, 6 → This is sulfur (S), Z = 16

You can work backwards too: given configuration 2, 8, 5 → 3 shells (Period 3), 5 valence electrons (Group 15) → phosphorus.

Why Electron Configuration Matters

Almost everything about chemistry follows from electron configuration:

Reactivity: Atoms with nearly full or nearly empty outer shells react most readily (trying to complete or empty the shell).

Bonding: Atoms share or transfer electrons to reach a stable configuration.

Periodic trends: Atomic radius, ionization energy, and electronegativity all follow from how tightly the nucleus holds the outer electrons.

Ion charge: Na loses 1 electron → Na⁺. Mg loses 2 → Mg²⁺. Cl gains 1 → Cl⁻. The charge tells you how many electrons were gained/lost to reach a noble gas configuration.
