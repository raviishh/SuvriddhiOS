Organic Chemistry

Part 5 — Alcohols

The Hydroxyl Group

Alcohols contain the –OH (hydroxyl) functional group attached to a carbon chain. The –OH group is polar, capable of hydrogen bonding, and responsible for most of alcohol's chemical reactivity. It's one of the most common functional groups in organic chemistry — from simple ethanol to complex sugars.

Primary alcohol: –OH on a carbon bonded to at most one other carbon. CH₃OH (methanol), C₂H₅OH (ethanol), propan-1-ol.

Secondary alcohol: –OH on a carbon bonded to two other carbons. Propan-2-ol.

Tertiary alcohol: –OH on a carbon bonded to three other carbons. 2-methylpropan-2-ol.

Classification matters because oxidation behaviour differs between primary, secondary, and tertiary alcohols.

Physical Properties

Hydrogen bonding between –OH groups means alcohols have unexpectedly high boiling points for their molecular mass. Ethanol (MW=46) boils at 78°C; propane (MW=44) boils at −42°C.

Miscibility with water: Short-chain alcohols (C1–C4) mix completely with water — the –OH forms H-bonds with water. As chain length increases, the non-polar hydrocarbon chain dominates and water solubility decreases.

Reactions of Alcohols

1. Combustion

All alcohols burn in excess oxygen to give CO₂ and H₂O. Alcohols are used as fuels — ethanol in biofuel blends.

C₂H₅OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O

2. Oxidation

Using oxidizing agents: acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ (orange → green) or acidified KMnO₄ (purple → colourless).

Primary alcohol → Aldehyde → Carboxylic acid

CH₃CH₂OH → CH₃CHO → CH₃COOH

(ethanol → ethanal → ethanoic acid)

Secondary alcohol → Ketone (cannot be oxidized further easily)

CH₃CHOHCH₃ → CH₃COCH₃    (propan-2-ol → propanone)

Tertiary alcohol → does NOT oxidize — no H on the carbon bearing –OH.

3. Dehydration

With concentrated H₂SO₄ or Al₂O₃ at high temperature, alcohols lose water to form alkenes.

C₂H₅OH → CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O    (ethanol → ethene)

The –OH and a neighbouring H are eliminated. This is how ethene is made from bio-ethanol.

4. Esterification

With a carboxylic acid + H₂SO₄ catalyst → ester + water (see Functional Groups lesson).

C₂H₅OH + CH₃COOH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O

Ethanol — The Most Important Alcohol

Two methods of production:

1. Fermentation: Glucose → ethanol + CO₂ using yeast enzymes (zymase).

C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂   (anaerobic, ~35°C, pH ~4–5)

Slow, renewable, batch process. Max ~15% alcohol (yeast killed by higher concentrations).

2. Hydration of ethene: CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH

Fast, continuous, non-renewable (ethene from crude oil cracking). High purity product.

Example: Distinguishing Aldehydes from Ketones

Both are products of alcohol oxidation, but they react differently with mild oxidizing agents:

Tollens' reagent (silver mirror test): Aldehyde reduces Ag⁺ → Ag (silver mirror forms). Ketone — no reaction.

Fehling's solution: Aldehyde reduces Cu²⁺ (blue) → Cu₂O (brick red precipitate). Ketone — no reaction.

Aldehydes can be oxidized further (to carboxylic acids); ketones cannot. This is why they behave differently with mild oxidizing agents — a key distinction in organic analysis.
