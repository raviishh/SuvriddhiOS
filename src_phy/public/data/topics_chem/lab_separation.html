Laboratory Techniques

Part 2 — Separation Techniques

Choosing the Right Technique

Separation techniques work by exploiting differences in physical properties between substances in a mixture. No new bonds are broken — these are all physical processes. The key is matching the technique to the property that differs between the components.

Filtration: Difference in particle size (solid vs dissolved). Insoluble solid stays on filter paper; solution passes through as filtrate.

Evaporation: Difference in volatility. Solvent boils away, leaving dissolved solid.

Distillation: Difference in boiling point. Component with lower BP evaporates first, condenses and is collected separately.

Crystallization: Difference in solubility at different temperatures. Cool saturated solution → pure crystals form.

Chromatography: Difference in how strongly components interact with stationary vs mobile phase.

Chromatography — Principle

All forms of chromatography work on the same principle: a mobile phase carries the mixture through a stationary phase. Components that interact more strongly with the stationary phase move more slowly; those that interact more with the mobile phase travel further.

Rf value (Retention factor):

Rf = distance travelled by spot / distance travelled by solvent front

Always between 0 and 1. Characteristic for a given compound under specific conditions (same solvent, same paper, same temperature).

Example: Calculating Rf

Solvent front travels 12.0 cm. Compound X travels 8.4 cm.

Rf(X) = 8.4 / 12.0 = 0.70

If a known compound has Rf = 0.70 under the same conditions, compound X is likely the same substance.

Rf values are used for identification — like a fingerprint for a compound in that solvent system. Using multiple solvents gives more certainty (two-way chromatography).

Types of Chromatography

Paper chromatography: Stationary phase = water absorbed in paper. Mobile phase = organic solvent. Used for amino acids, plant pigments, food dyes.

Thin-layer chromatography (TLC): Stationary phase = silica on aluminium plate. Faster than paper; used in organic synthesis to monitor reactions.

Column chromatography: Large-scale separation. Sample moves through silica column with solvent. Different compounds elute (exit) at different times. Used to purify reaction products.

Gas chromatography (GC): Mobile phase = inert gas. Sample is vaporized and carried through a column. Each compound has a characteristic retention time. Used for volatile organic compounds, breath analysis, food flavour analysis.

HPLC (High-performance liquid chromatography): Solvent pumped at high pressure through fine column. Very high resolution. Used in pharmaceutical analysis, protein separation.

Crystallization

Used to obtain a pure solid from a solution containing impurities.

1. Dissolve impure solid in minimum hot solvent (usually water)

2. Filter while hot to remove insoluble impurities

3. Allow to cool slowly — the pure compound crystallizes out (solubility decreases as temp drops)

4. Filter off crystals; wash with cold solvent to remove soluble impurities

5. Dry crystals (in oven or over silica gel)

Slow cooling = larger, purer crystals. Fast cooling = small, impure crystals. Temperature gradient drives the process.

Gravimetric Analysis

Determines the amount of a substance by converting it to an insoluble product, isolating it, and weighing it precisely.

Example: finding Cl⁻ in a sample.

Dissolve in water → add excess AgNO₃ → AgCl precipitate forms → filter → wash → dry → weigh

From m(AgCl), calculate n(AgCl) → n(Cl⁻) → m(Cl⁻) → % Cl in original sample

Relies on complete precipitation. Any dissolved AgCl (Ksp = 1.8×10⁻¹⁰) is a source of error. Excess Ag⁺ pushes the equilibrium further right, minimizing dissolved AgCl.
