Stoichiometry

Part 3 — Limiting Reagent and Percentage Yield

When Reactants Run Out

In real chemistry, you rarely mix reactants in perfect stoichiometric ratios. One reactant runs out before the other — and once it's gone, the reaction stops, no matter how much of the other reactant remains. The reactant that runs out first is the limiting reagent.

The Limiting Reagent

The limiting reagent is the reactant that is completely consumed first. It determines the maximum amount of product that can form. The other reactant is in excess — some is left over at the end.

Finding the Limiting Reagent

Method: Calculate moles needed vs moles available

1. Convert all given masses to moles.

2. Divide each by its stoichiometric coefficient.

3. The reactant with the smallest result is the limiting reagent.

Example: Finding the Limiting Reagent

10 g of H₂ and 60 g of O₂ are mixed.   2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

Moles: n(H₂) = 10/2 = 5 mol     n(O₂) = 60/32 = 1.875 mol

Divide by coefficient: H₂: 5/2 = 2.5     O₂: 1.875/1 = 1.875

Smaller value = limiting reagent: O₂

n(H₂O) = 2 × n(O₂) = 2 × 1.875 = 3.75 mol

m(H₂O) = 3.75 × 18 = 67.5 g

H₂ remaining = 5 − (2 × 1.875) = 5 − 3.75 = 1.25 mol H₂ left over

Once you identify the limiting reagent, ALL subsequent calculations are based on the moles of the limiting reagent only. Ignore the excess.

Theoretical Yield vs Actual Yield

Stoichiometry gives you the theoretical yield — the maximum possible amount of product based on the limiting reagent. In practice, you almost always get less.

Theoretical yield: The maximum mass of product calculated from the stoichiometry. Assumes 100% of limiting reagent converts to product.

Actual yield: The mass of product actually obtained in the experiment.

Percentage yield = (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) × 100%

Why is % yield always <100%? Several reasons:

• Reaction may not go to completion (reversible reactions reach equilibrium)

• Side reactions produce unwanted products

• Product is lost during transfer, filtration, or crystallization

• Impure reactants mean fewer effective moles than calculated

Percentage yield >100% would violate conservation of mass — impossible in a real experiment (and means you made an error).

Example: Percentage Yield of Aspirin

Theoretical yield calculated from stoichiometry: 5.40 g of aspirin.

Actual yield after filtering, drying, and weighing: 4.21 g.

% yield = (4.21 / 5.40) × 100 = 78.0%

A yield of 78% is quite good for an undergraduate synthesis. Industrial processes are optimized to push yields as high as possible — even a 1% improvement on a million-tonne scale saves enormous amounts of money and reduces waste.

Atom Economy

Percentage yield measures how much product you actually got. Atom economy measures how efficiently the reaction uses its atoms — how much of the reactant mass ends up in the desired product rather than in byproducts.

% Atom economy = (Molar mass of desired product / Sum of molar masses of all products) × 100%

High atom economy = most atoms end up in the useful product = less waste = greener chemistry.

Addition reactions (like alkene + H₂ → alkane) have 100% atom economy — all atoms end up in the product.

Substitution reactions produce byproducts, lowering atom economy.
