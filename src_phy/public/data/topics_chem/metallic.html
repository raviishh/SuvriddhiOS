Chemical Bonding

Part 3 — Metallic Bonding

The Electron Sea Model

Metals don't bond the same way as ionic or covalent compounds. Metal atoms have low ionization energies — they give up their outer electrons easily. In a metal, all atoms release their valence electrons into a shared pool. What's left is a lattice of positive metal ions (cations) sitting in a "sea" of delocalized electrons that move freely throughout the entire structure.

The Metallic Bond

The metallic bond is the electrostatic attraction between the lattice of positive metal ions and the sea of delocalized (free) electrons surrounding them. It is non-directional — the electrons are shared across the entire structure, not between specific atom pairs.

Properties Explained by the Model

Electrical conductivity: The delocalized electrons can move freely through the lattice in response to a potential difference. Metals conduct electricity in both solid and liquid states.

Thermal conductivity: Free electrons also transfer kinetic energy rapidly through the metal. Metals feel cold because they conduct heat away from your hand quickly.

Malleability (can be hammered into sheets): The non-directional electron sea allows layers of metal ions to slide past each other without breaking the bond. The sea of electrons just shifts to accommodate the new arrangement.

Ductility (can be drawn into wires): Same reason — layers can slide. Metals can be stretched into thin wires.

Lustre (shiny appearance): Free electrons absorb and re-emit light across a wide range of frequencies, giving metals their characteristic shine.

High melting points: The metallic bond is strong. Lots of energy needed to free the ions from the electron sea. Exceptions: Group 1 metals (1 valence electron, weak metallic bond) have relatively low melting points.

Comparing Bond Types

Ionic: Metal + non-metal. Electron transfer. Lattice of ions. High MP. Conducts when molten/dissolved.

Covalent (simple): Non-metal + non-metal. Electron sharing. Discrete molecules. Low MP. Usually doesn't conduct.

Covalent (giant): Diamond, SiO₂. Covalent network. Extremely high MP. Doesn't conduct (usually).

Metallic: Metal + metal. Electron sea. Lattice of cations. High MP. Always conducts (solid or liquid).

Example: Comparing Na, NaCl, and Diamond

Sodium (Na): Metallic bonding. Soft (weak metallic bond — only 1 valence e⁻), good conductor, MP = 98°C.

NaCl: Ionic. Hard, brittle, MP = 801°C. Conducts only when molten/dissolved.

Diamond (C): Giant covalent. Extremely hard (each C bonded to 4 others), MP > 3500°C. Does not conduct.

Three completely different structures and properties — all determined by their bonding type.
