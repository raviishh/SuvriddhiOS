Organic Chemistry

Part 3 — Alkenes

The Double Bond Changes Everything

Alkenes contain at least one C=C double bond. This makes them fundamentally more reactive than alkanes. The double bond consists of a strong sigma (σ) bond and a weaker pi (π) bond — the π bond sits above and below the C–C axis, and its electrons are more exposed and easier to attack.

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ

Ethene: C₂H₄    Propene: C₃H₆    But-1-ene: C₄H₈    But-2-ene: C₄H₈

Addition Reactions

The π bond is the reactive site. Electrophiles (electron-poor species) are attracted to the electron-rich double bond and add across it, breaking the π bond and forming two new single bonds. The product is always a saturated compound (no more double bond).

Hydrogenation (H₂ addition)

C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆    (ethene → ethane)

Conditions: Ni catalyst, ~150°C

Used industrially to convert unsaturated vegetable oils into saturated fats (margarine production). Atom economy = 100%.

Halogenation (Br₂ or Cl₂ addition)

C₂H₄ + Br₂ → CH₂BrCH₂Br    (1,2-dibromoethane)

Orange/brown bromine water decolorizes — used as a test for C=C double bonds.

No catalyst needed. Alkanes don't react with Br₂ at room temperature (no color change) — this distinguishes them from alkenes.

Hydration (H₂O addition)

C₂H₄ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH    (ethanol)

Conditions: H₃PO₄ catalyst, 300°C, 60 atm (steam)

Industrial method for making ethanol. Reversible reaction — equilibrium favors ethanol at lower temperatures but reaction is too slow; compromise conditions used.

Hydrohalogenation (HBr or HCl addition)

C₂H₄ + HBr → CH₃CH₂Br    (bromoethane)

For unsymmetrical alkenes (propene etc.), Markovnikov's rule applies: H adds to the carbon that already has more H atoms. The halogen goes to the more substituted carbon.

Polymerization

Alkenes can link together in long chains through addition polymerization. Each monomer's double bond opens and forms bonds with the next monomer. No atoms are lost — everything ends up in the polymer.

n(CH₂=CH₂) → −(CH₂–CH₂)ₙ−    poly(ethene) / polyethylene (PE)

n(CH₂=CHCH₃) → −(CH₂–CHCH₃)ₙ−    poly(propene) / polypropylene (PP)

n(CH₂=CHCl) → −(CH₂–CHCl)ₙ−    poly(chloroethene) / PVC

Properties of the polymer depend on monomer, branching, and cross-linking. LDPE (low density) is branched and flexible; HDPE (high density) is linear and rigid.

Alkene vs Alkane — Quick Comparison

Test with Br₂(aq): Alkene decolorizes (reacts). Alkane — no change.

Test with KMnO₄(aq): Alkene decolorizes purple permanganate (gets oxidized). Alkane — no change.

Combustion: Both burn, but alkenes produce sootier flames (higher C:H ratio).

The C=C is the source of all alkene reactivity. Every addition reaction follows the same logic: something adds across the double bond, breaking the π bond and making two new σ bonds.
