Acids and Bases

Part 3 — The pH Scale

Measuring Acidity

The pH scale is a way to express the concentration of H⁺ ions in a solution on a manageable scale. Without it, you'd have to write numbers like 0.00001 mol/dm³ all the time.

pH Definition

pH = −log₁₀[H⁺]

[H⁺] is in mol/dm³. The negative log means: higher H⁺ concentration → lower pH.

The scale is logarithmic: a change of 1 pH unit represents a 10× change in H⁺ concentration. pH 3 is 10 times more acidic than pH 4, and 100 times more acidic than pH 5.

pH → [H⁺]:   [H⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ

[H⁺] → pH:   pH = −log₁₀[H⁺]

Example: [H⁺] = 0.01 mol/dm³ = 10⁻² → pH = 2

Example: [H⁺] = 1×10⁻⁸ mol/dm³ → pH = 8

Autoionization of Water

Pure water is not entirely un-ionized. A tiny fraction of water molecules transfer protons to each other:

H₂O(l) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

At 25°C: [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 1×10⁻⁷ mol/dm³

pH = −log(10⁻⁷) = 7 → neutral

The product [H⁺][OH⁻] = Kw = 1×10⁻¹⁴ mol²/dm⁶ at 25°C (the ionic product of water).

This means: if [H⁺] goes up (more acidic), [OH⁻] must go down proportionally, and vice versa.

Example: Finding [H⁺] and [OH⁻] from pH

A solution has pH = 3.

[H⁺] = 10⁻³ = 1×10⁻³ mol/dm³

[OH⁻] = Kw / [H⁺] = 10⁻¹⁴ / 10⁻³ = 10⁻¹¹ mol/dm³

Solution is acidic (pH < 7). ✓

For strong acids: [H⁺] = concentration of acid (fully ionized). 0.01 mol/dm³ HCl → [H⁺] = 0.01 → pH = 2. For weak acids, [H⁺] < concentration (partial ionization only).

Measuring pH

Universal indicator: A mixture of indicators that gives a range of colours across the pH scale. Good for estimating pH to ±1 unit. Colour chart is provided and matched visually.

pH meter: Electronic probe that gives a precise numerical pH reading. Much more accurate than indicators.

Specific indicators: Litmus, phenolphthalein, methyl orange — each has a narrow pH range where it changes colour. Used in titrations where you need a sharp endpoint.

pH of common substances: Stomach acid ~1.5–3.5, lemon juice ~2, cola ~2.5, coffee ~5, pure water 7, blood ~7.4, baking soda ~8.3, ammonia solution ~11, bleach ~12.5, NaOH ~13–14.

Blood is maintained between pH 7.35–7.45 by buffer systems — a change of 0.4 units either way is life-threatening.
