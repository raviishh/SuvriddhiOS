Chemical Equilibrium

Dynamic Equilibrium and Le Chatelier's Principle

Not All Reactions Go to Completion

Some reactions are irreversible — they go essentially to completion (like burning wood). But many reactions are reversible: as products form, they can react with each other to regenerate the original reactants. Over time, the rates of the forward and reverse reactions become equal, and the system reaches dynamic equilibrium.

Dynamic Equilibrium

At equilibrium, both the forward and reverse reactions are still occurring — but at the same rate. As a result, the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant over time.

"Dynamic" because the reactions are still happening. "Equilibrium" because there's no net change in concentrations.

Shown with a double arrow: A + B ⇌ C + D

The Equilibrium Constant (Kc)

For any equilibrium: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ

Products over reactants, each raised to stoichiometric coefficients.

Kc is constant at a given temperature. Changing concentration, pressure doesn't change Kc — only temperature does.

Kc >> 1: equilibrium favors products.   Kc << 1: equilibrium favors reactants.

Le Chatelier's Principle

If a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to oppose the disturbance and restore equilibrium. This is one of the most powerful predictive tools in chemistry.

Increase [reactant]: System shifts right (toward products) to use up the added reactant.

Decrease [product]: System shifts right to replace the removed product.

Increase pressure (gases): System shifts toward the side with fewer moles of gas.

Decrease pressure: System shifts toward the side with more moles of gas.

Increase temperature: System shifts in the endothermic direction (absorbs the added heat).

Decrease temperature: System shifts in the exothermic direction.

Add catalyst: Reaches equilibrium faster — does NOT shift the position of equilibrium. Kc unchanged.

Case Study: The Haber Process

N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)   ΔH = −92 kJ mol⁻¹ (exothermic)

Pressure: Left side: 4 mol gas. Right side: 2 mol gas. High pressure (200 atm) → shifts right → more NH₃. ✓

Temperature: Reaction is exothermic. Low temp favors NH₃ (shifts right). But rate is too slow at low temp — needs a catalyst.

Compromise: ~450°C with iron catalyst. Gives acceptable yield (~15% NH₃) at a practical rate. NH₃ is continuously removed to shift equilibrium right.

The Haber process is a perfect example of industrial compromise: you can't have maximum yield AND maximum rate simultaneously. The chosen conditions balance economics, rate, and yield.

Kc vs Kp: For gas-phase equilibria, the equilibrium constant can be expressed in terms of partial pressures (Kp) instead of concentrations. At IB SL you mainly use Kc, but know that Kp exists and is related to Kc by Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn.
