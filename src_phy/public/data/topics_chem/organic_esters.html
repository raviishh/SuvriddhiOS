Organic Chemistry

Esters

The –COO– Linkage

Esters contain the ester linkage (–COO–) — a carbonyl group (C=O) bonded to an oxygen that connects to a carbon chain. They're formed when a carboxylic acid reacts with an alcohol in a process called esterification. Esters are responsible for most of the pleasant fruity and floral smells we encounter — banana, pear, strawberry, rum, and nail polish remover are all esters.

Esterification

Carboxylic acid + Alcohol ⇌ Ester + Water

CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O

Conditions: Concentrated H₂SO₄ as catalyst, heat (~60–80°C), reflux condenser to prevent evaporation.

The reaction is reversible — equilibrium is established. To improve yield: use excess of one reactant, remove water as it forms, or distil off the ester product.

Naming Esters

The name has two parts: the alkyl group from the alcohol (first), and the -anoate name from the acid (second). The acid name loses its "-ic acid" ending and gets "-ate" instead.

Methanol + Ethanoic acid → Methyl ethanoate (CH₃COOCH₃) — pear drops smell

Ethanol + Ethanoic acid → Ethyl ethanoate (CH₃COOC₂H₅) — nail polish remover

Pentanol + Ethanoic acid → Pentyl ethanoate — banana flavouring

Octanol + Ethanoic acid → Octyl ethanoate — orange flavouring

Ethanol + Butanoic acid → Ethyl butanoate — pineapple flavouring

Hydrolysis of Esters

Esterification is reversible — esters can be broken back down (hydrolyzed) into the acid and alcohol. There are two types of hydrolysis:

Acid hydrolysis: Ester + water + dilute acid catalyst → carboxylic acid + alcohol

CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH

Reversible — gives back the original acid and alcohol.

Base hydrolysis (saponification): Ester + NaOH → carboxylate salt + alcohol

CH₃COOC₂H₅ + NaOH → CH₃COONa + C₂H₅OH

Irreversible — produces the sodium salt of the acid (not the free acid). This is exactly how soap is made — triglyceride esters (fats) are hydrolyzed with NaOH to give glycerol and fatty acid salts (soap).

Uses of Esters

Food flavourings and fragrances: Most artificial fruit flavours are esters. Isoamyl acetate = banana. Ethyl butanoate = pineapple. Used in sweets, drinks, perfumes.

Solvents: Ethyl ethanoate is widely used as a solvent in glues, nail polish remover, and chemical synthesis — it dissolves many organic compounds and evaporates cleanly.

Plasticizers: Certain esters (like phthalates) are added to PVC to make it flexible.

Biodiesel: Made by transesterification of vegetable oils (triglycerides) with methanol. Produces fatty acid methyl esters (FAMEs) — a renewable fuel.

Polyesters: PET (used in plastic bottles and clothing fibres like Dacron/Terylene) is a polymer made from repeated ester linkages between diacids and diols.

Example: Identifying Reactants from an Ester

Given: propyl methanoate. What acid and alcohol were used to make it?

Name breakdown: propyl (from propanol, C₃H₇OH) + methanoate (from methanoic acid, HCOOH)

Reactants: propan-1-ol + methanoic acid

Equation: HCOOH + C₃H₇OH ⇌ HCOOC₃H₇ + H₂O

To go backwards from an ester name: the alkyl part → add -ol to get the alcohol. The -anoate part → change to -anoic acid. Split the –COO– bond to get your two reactants.

Esters vs carboxylic acids: Despite similar molecular masses, esters have significantly lower boiling points than carboxylic acids. Carboxylic acids form hydrogen-bonded dimers; esters cannot form H-bonds with themselves (no O–H group) — only weaker dipole-dipole forces. This is why esters are more volatile and have stronger smells.
