Redox Reactions

Part 3 — Real-World Applications

Electrochemical Cells

A battery is a device that converts chemical energy into electrical energy through a spontaneous redox reaction. The two half-reactions are separated into different compartments — electrodes — and electrons flow through an external circuit from the anode to the cathode.

Anode and Cathode

Anode: Where oxidation occurs. Negative electrode in a galvanic cell (electrons leave from here).

Cathode: Where reduction occurs. Positive electrode in a galvanic cell (electrons arrive here).

Memory: Red Cat An Ox — Reduction at Cathode, Anode is Oxidation.

Electroplating

Electroplating uses an external electrical supply (non-spontaneous) to drive a redox reaction, depositing a thin layer of metal onto an object.

The object to be plated is the cathode (reduction occurs, metal deposits).

The plating metal is the anode (oxidation occurs, anode dissolves into solution).

The electrolyte solution contains ions of the plating metal.

Example — chrome plating: Object (cathode) in CrO₃ solution. Cr⁶⁺ + 6e⁻ → Cr. Chromium metal deposits on the object, giving a shiny, corrosion-resistant coating.

Used on: car bumpers, taps, cutlery, circuit boards, jewellery.

Corrosion — Redox in the Worst Way

Rusting is an electrochemical process. Iron acts as an anode in small local cells on its surface, particularly where stresses or impurities exist.

Oxidation: Fe(s) → Fe²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻   (at the anode — iron dissolves)

Reduction: O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻(aq)   (at the cathode)

Fe²⁺ and OH⁻ combine and further oxidize to form Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (hydrated iron(III) oxide — rust).

Conditions needed for rusting: Both oxygen AND water are required. Neither alone causes rust.

Preventing Corrosion

Physical barriers: Paint, oil, grease — prevent water and oxygen reaching iron.

Galvanizing: Coating with zinc. Even when the coating scratches, zinc acts as a sacrificial anode — it oxidizes preferentially, protecting the iron.

Alloying: Stainless steel (iron + chromium + nickel). Chromium forms Cr₂O₃ — a thin, protective oxide layer.

Sacrificial protection: Attaching a more reactive metal (Mg, Zn) to the iron. The more reactive metal oxidizes instead of the iron.

Example: Why Does the Hull of a Ship Use Zinc Blocks?

Steel ship hulls are in constant contact with oxygenated seawater — ideal rusting conditions. Zinc blocks are bolted to the hull. Zinc is higher in the reactivity series than iron, so it preferentially oxidizes:

Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻   (zinc corrodes, iron is protected)

The zinc blocks are replaced periodically when they've corroded away. This is far cheaper than replacing the ship's hull.

Sacrificial protection is elegant: use the more reactive metal as the "victim" to protect the valuable structure. The same principle is used on pipelines, oil rigs, and bridges.
