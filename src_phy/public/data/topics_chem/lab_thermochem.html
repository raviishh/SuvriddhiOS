Part 3 — Thermochemistry and Energy Changes

Energy and Chemical Reactions

Every chemical reaction involves energy changes — bonds break (requiring energy) and new bonds form (releasing energy). The overall energy change determines whether a reaction releases or absorbs heat.

Exothermic vs Endothermic

Exothermic: Reaction releases heat to surroundings. System loses energy. Temperature of surroundings increases. ΔH is negative (−).

Examples: combustion, neutralization, oxidation of metals, respiration.

Endothermic: Reaction absorbs heat from surroundings. System gains energy. Temperature of surroundings decreases. ΔH is positive (+).

Examples: thermal decomposition, dissolving ammonium nitrate, photosynthesis.

Measuring Enthalpy Changes — Calorimetry

The enthalpy change (ΔH) of a reaction is the heat exchanged at constant pressure. In the lab, we measure it by tracking the temperature change of a known mass of solution.

q = mcΔT

q = heat energy transferred (J)

m = mass of solution (g) — usually assumed to be mass of water present

c = specific heat capacity of water = 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹

ΔT = temperature change (°C or K, same magnitude)

ΔH (kJ/mol) = −q / n    (negative because if solution heats up, reaction released energy)

Example: Enthalpy of Neutralization

50 cm³ of 1.0 mol/dm³ HCl + 50 cm³ of 1.0 mol/dm³ NaOH mixed in polystyrene cup.

Temperature rises from 20.0°C to 26.4°C. ΔT = 6.4°C

q = 100 × 4.18 × 6.4 = 2675 J = 2.675 kJ   (m = 100 g total)

n(HCl) = 0.050 × 1.0 = 0.050 mol   (limiting reagent)

ΔH = −2.675 / 0.050 = −53.5 kJ/mol

Literature value: −57.1 kJ/mol. Difference due to heat loss to surroundings.

The negative sign confirms exothermic. The discrepancy with literature is expected — a polystyrene cup is not a perfect calorimeter. Sources of error: heat loss, density of solution assumed = 1 g/cm³, specific heat capacity assumed same as water.

Hess's Law

The enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the route taken — only the initial and final states matter. This allows us to calculate ΔH for reactions we can't measure directly.

Hess's Law

ΔH(overall) = ΔH₁ + ΔH₂ + ΔH₃ + ...

If you reverse a step, change the sign of ΔH. If you multiply a step by n, multiply ΔH by n.

This is a consequence of the conservation of energy — enthalpy is a state function.

Standard enthalpy of combustion (ΔHc°): Heat released when 1 mol of substance burns completely in excess O₂ under standard conditions. Always negative.

Standard enthalpy of formation (ΔHf°): Heat change when 1 mol of compound is formed from its elements in standard states. Elements in standard state: ΔHf° = 0 by definition.

Bond enthalpy: Energy needed to break 1 mol of a specific bond in the gas phase. All bond enthalpies are positive (breaking bonds always requires energy).

ΔH(reaction) ≈ Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds formed)

If more energy released forming bonds than breaking: ΔH negative → exothermic. ✓

Why bond enthalpy calculations are approximate: Bond enthalpies are average values across many different compounds. The actual energy of a C–H bond in methane differs slightly from a C–H bond in ethane. Using average values introduces error. ΔHf° and ΔHc° values from Hess's Law cycles are more accurate.
