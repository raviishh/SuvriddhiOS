Acids and Bases

Part 2 — Neutralization and Salts

Neutralization

When an acid and a base react together, the H⁺ ions from the acid combine with the OH⁻ ions from the base to form water. The remaining ions form a salt. This is a neutralization reaction.

The Ionic Equation for Neutralization

Regardless of which acid and base you use, the core reaction is always:

H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)

This is the net ionic equation. The salt formed comes from the spectator ions — the cation from the base and the anion from the acid.

Full equation format:

Acid + Base → Salt + Water

HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)

H₂SO₄(aq) + 2KOH(aq) → K₂SO₄(aq) + 2H₂O(l)

HNO₃(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaNO₃(aq) + H₂O(l)

Naming Salts

The name of the salt comes from: [cation from base] [anion from acid]

Nature of the Salt Formed

The pH of a salt solution depends on the relative strengths of the acid and base used to make it:

Strong acid + Strong base → Neutral salt (pH ≈ 7)
e.g. HCl + NaOH → NaCl. Neither Na⁺ nor Cl⁻ hydrolyses water significantly.

Strong acid + Weak base → Acidic salt (pH < 7)
e.g. HCl + NH₃ → NH₄Cl. The NH₄⁺ ion donates protons to water: NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺

Weak acid + Strong base → Basic salt (pH > 7)
e.g. CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa. The CH₃COO⁻ ion accepts protons from water: CH₃COO⁻ + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COOH + OH⁻

Acid Reactions with Metals and Carbonates

Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas

Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)

Mg(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → MgSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)

Test for H₂: burns with a squeaky pop with a lit splint.

Acid + Carbonate → Salt + Water + Carbon dioxide

CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)

Na₂CO₃(aq) + H₂SO₄(aq) → Na₂SO₄(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)

Test for CO₂: turns limewater milky (forms CaCO₃ precipitate).

Example: Making Copper Sulfate Crystals

Copper doesn't react with dilute H₂SO₄ (below hydrogen in reactivity series). Instead:

CuO(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → CuSO₄(aq) + H₂O(l)

Add excess CuO (black powder) to hot H₂SO₄. Filter off excess CuO. Evaporate to crystallize blue CuSO₄·5H₂O.

Excess base is used to ensure all the acid reacts — then it's removed by filtration. The product is a pure salt solution.

Indicators

Indicators are weak acids (or bases) whose conjugate forms have a different colour. They change colour at specific pH values.

Litmus: Red in acid (pH <7), blue in alkali (pH >7). Not sharp enough for titrations.

Phenolphthalein: Colourless in acid, pink in alkali. Changes at pH ~8.2–10. Good for strong acid/strong base titrations.

Methyl orange: Red in acid, yellow in alkali. Changes at pH ~3.1–4.4. Good for strong acid/weak base titrations.
