Acids and Bases

Part 1 — Defining Acids and Bases

The Challenge of Definition

What makes something an acid? Sour taste? Corrodes metals? Turns litmus red? These are properties, not explanations. Chemists needed a definition based on what acids and bases actually do at the molecular level. Three definitions have been developed, each more general than the last.

Arrhenius Theory (1887)

Acid: Produces H⁺ ions when dissolved in water.

HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)

Base: Produces OH⁻ ions when dissolved in water.

NaOH(aq) → Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

Limitation: requires water. Cannot explain why NH₃ is basic (it has no OH⁻), or why reactions between dry HCl gas and dry NH₃ gas produce NH₄Cl.

Brønsted–Lowry Theory (1923)

Acid: A proton (H⁺) donor.

Base: A proton (H⁺) acceptor.

This works without water. Example:

HCl + NH₃ → NH₄⁺ + Cl⁻

HCl donates H⁺ (acid). NH₃ accepts H⁺ (base).

The concept of conjugate acid-base pairs emerges: when an acid donates H⁺, it becomes a conjugate base; when a base accepts H⁺, it becomes a conjugate acid.

Strong vs Weak Acids and Bases

Not all acids donate protons with equal ease. The strength of an acid refers to how completely it ionizes in water — not to its concentration or corrosiveness.

Strong acid: Ionizes (almost) completely in water. Essentially no HX molecules remain — all H⁺ and X⁻.

Strong acids: HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄

HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻    (one-way arrow — goes to completion)

Weak acid: Partially ionizes. An equilibrium is established with most molecules remaining un-ionized.

Weak acids: CH₃COOH (acetic acid), HF, H₂CO₃, H₃PO₄

CH₃COOH ⇌ H⁺ + CH₃COO⁻    (double arrow — equilibrium)

Strong base: Fully ionizes. NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂.

Weak base: Partially ionizes. NH₃(aq) + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻

Concentration ≠ Strength: A concentrated weak acid can have more H⁺ ions than a dilute strong acid. "Strong" and "weak" refer only to the degree of ionization, not the amount dissolved.

Properties of Acids

• Sour taste (e.g. lemon juice — citric acid; vinegar — acetic acid)

• Turn blue litmus red; turn universal indicator red/orange/yellow

• React with reactive metals to produce H₂ gas:   Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂

• React with metal carbonates to produce CO₂:   CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂

• React with bases in neutralization reactions

• Corrosive — handle with care

Properties of Bases

• Bitter taste; slippery/soapy feel (due to saponification of skin oils)

• Turn red litmus blue; turn universal indicator blue/purple

• React with acids (neutralization)

• Caustic (alkalis like NaOH can burn skin)

• Soluble bases are called alkalis (NaOH, KOH, NH₃ solution)
