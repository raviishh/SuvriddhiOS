Physical and Chemical Changes

Matter and Its Nature — Part 2

The Core Distinction

Every change in the physical world is either a physical change or a chemical change. The difference comes down to one question: has a new substance been formed?

Physical Change

The substance changes in form or appearance, but its chemical composition remains exactly the same. No new bonds are formed or broken at the molecular level. Usually reversible.

Examples: melting, freezing, boiling, dissolving, cutting, bending, crushing.

Chemical Change

One or more new substances are formed with different properties. Existing chemical bonds are broken and new ones form. Usually irreversible.

Examples: burning, rusting, cooking an egg, digestion, neutralization, photosynthesis.

Signs of a Chemical Change

You can't always see bonds breaking, but these observable clues strongly suggest a chemical change has occurred:

🌡️ Temperature change — heat released (exothermic) or absorbed (endothermic)

💡 Light produced — flames, glowing, bioluminescence

🫧 Gas produced — bubbles that aren't from boiling

🎨 Colour change — a new substance with different optical properties formed

⬇️ Precipitate formed — an insoluble solid appears in a clear solution

👃 New smell — new volatile compounds formed

Caution: Dissolving can look like a chemical change (the solid "disappears") but it's physical — you can evaporate the water to get the solid back. Colour change also happens when a dye dissolves — no chemical change. Always look for multiple signs together.

Elements, Compounds, and Mixtures

Understanding physical vs chemical changes also helps us classify matter itself.

Element: One type of atom only. Cannot be broken down by chemical means. Fe, Cu, O₂, N₂ are all elements.

Compound: Two or more elements chemically bonded in fixed ratios. Has completely different properties from the elements it came from. H₂O, NaCl, CO₂.

Mixture: Two or more substances physically combined. Each keeps its own properties. Can be separated by physical methods.

Separating Mixtures — Physical Methods

Because mixtures are held together physically (not chemically), physical techniques can separate them:

Filtration: Separates insoluble solid from liquid. Sand from water.

Evaporation: Removes solvent to leave dissolved solid. Salt from salt water.

Distillation: Separates liquids with different boiling points. Alcohol from water.

Chromatography: Separates substances with different solubilities in a solvent. Food dyes.

Magnetism: Separates magnetic materials. Iron filings from sand.

Example: Salt Water — Is Dissolving Physical or Chemical?

When NaCl dissolves in water, the ions separate and spread through the water — but no new substance is formed. The Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are still Na⁺ and Cl⁻. Evaporate the water and you get salt back.

Physical change.

Compare: when sodium metal is dropped into water:

2Na(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g)

New substances (NaOH and H₂ gas) are formed. Heat and light are released. Chemical change.

Same element (sodium), completely different outcome depending on what it's interacting with. Context matters.

Example: Cooking an Egg

Raw egg white is a clear liquid; cooked egg white is an opaque solid. The heat causes proteins in the egg to permanently unfold and cross-link — new bonds form. You cannot "uncook" an egg.

Irreversibility is one of the strongest indicators of a chemical change.
