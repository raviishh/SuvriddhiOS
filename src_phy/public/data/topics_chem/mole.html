Stoichiometry

Part 1 — The Mole

The Counting Problem

A single atom of carbon has a mass of about 2 × 10⁻²³ g. You can't weigh that on any lab scale. Chemists needed a way to count atoms in bulk — a unit that bridges the atomic scale and the real-world scale. That unit is the mole.

Avogadro's Number

One mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, ions — whatever the entity is).

This number (Nₐ) was chosen so that 1 mole of ¹²C atoms has a mass of exactly 12 g. This links atomic mass units (amu) to grams in a practical way.

6.022 × 10²³ is unimaginably large. A mole of grains of sand would cover Earth's land surface to a depth of several kilometres.

Molar Mass

The molar mass of a substance is the mass of one mole of it, in g/mol. Numerically it equals the relative atomic/molecular mass from the periodic table.

Working out molar mass:

For an element: M = Aᵣ in g/mol.   Carbon: M = 12 g/mol

For a compound: sum all atoms.   H₂O: 2(1) + 16 = 18 g/mol

NaCl: 23 + 35.5 = 58.5 g/mol

H₂SO₄: 2(1) + 32 + 4(16) = 98 g/mol

n = m / M

n = amount (mol), m = mass (g), M = molar mass (g/mol)

Rearranges to:   m = n × M   or   M = m / n

Example: Moles in 49 g of H₂SO₄

M(H₂SO₄) = 2(1) + 32 + 4(16) = 98 g/mol

n = 49 / 98 = 0.5 mol

Number of molecules = 0.5 × 6.022 × 10²³ = 3.011 × 10²³

The mole links mass (something you can weigh) to particle count (what you need for chemical ratios). It's the central conversion in all stoichiometry.

Molar Volume of Gases

At standard temperature and pressure (STP: 0°C, 100 kPa), one mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 dm³. At room temperature (~25°C), use 24.0 dm³/mol.

n = V / Vₘ

V = volume in dm³, Vₘ = molar volume (22.4 or 24.0 dm³/mol)

1 dm³ = 1 litre = 1000 cm³. This molar volume applies to all ideal gases regardless of what the gas is — same number of moles always takes the same volume at the same conditions.

Concentration of Solutions

c = n / V

c = concentration (mol/dm³), n = moles, V = volume in dm³

Convert cm³ → dm³ by ÷ 1000.   250 cm³ = 0.250 dm³

Example: Making 500 cm³ of 0.2 mol/dm³ NaOH

n = c × V = 0.2 × 0.500 = 0.1 mol

m = n × M = 0.1 × 40 = 4.0 g of NaOH

Dissolve 4.0 g NaOH and make up to exactly 500 cm³ in a volumetric flask.
