Projectile Motion

Basic Definition

Projectile motion describes the motion of an object launched into the air and subject only to the force of gravity. The path followed is called a trajectory, and it takes the shape of a parabola.

Projectile: Any object that is given an initial velocity and then follows a path determined entirely by gravity (and air resistance, if considered). Examples include a thrown ball, a fired bullet, or a launched rocket in free flight.

Key Principle: Independence of Motion

The most important idea in projectile motion is that horizontal and vertical motion are completely independent of each other.

Horizontal vs Vertical Components

Horizontal: No force acts horizontally (ignoring air resistance), so horizontal velocity remains constant throughout the flight.

Vertical: Gravity acts downward at g = 9.8 m/s², so vertical velocity changes continuously — the object decelerates going up and accelerates coming down.

Example: Drop a ball and throw a ball horizontally at the same instant from the same height. Both hit the ground at the same time, because vertical motion is identical for both.

Equations of Motion

Horizontal Motion (constant velocity):

x = vx t

Where:

  • x = horizontal displacement
  • vx = horizontal velocity = v0 cos(θ)
  • t = time

Vertical Motion (uniform acceleration due to gravity):

y = vy t − ½ g t²

vy,final = vy − g t

Where:

  • y = vertical displacement
  • vy = initial vertical velocity = v0 sin(θ)
  • g = acceleration due to gravity = 9.8 m/s²
  • t = time

Key Derived Quantities

Time of Flight:

T = 2 v0 sin(θ) / g

The total time the projectile spends in the air. It is twice the time to reach maximum height, since the trajectory is symmetric.

Maximum Height:

H = v0² sin²(θ) / (2g)

Reached when vertical velocity = 0. Found by setting vy,final = 0 and solving for t, then substituting into the vertical displacement equation.

Horizontal Range:

R = v0² sin(2θ) / g

The total horizontal distance covered. Maximum range occurs at θ = 45°, since sin(2 × 45°) = sin(90°) = 1.

Angle Symmetry

Complementary launch angles (e.g. 30° and 60°) produce the same horizontal range. This is because sin(2θ) = sin(180° − 2θ), so sin(2 × 30°) = sin(2 × 60°) = sin(60°).

Example

Example: Ball Launched at 30 m/s at 45°

A ball is launched at v0 = 30 m/s at θ = 45°. Find range, max height, and time of flight.

Solution:

vx = 30 cos(45°) ≈ 21.2 m/s

vy = 30 sin(45°) ≈ 21.2 m/s

T = 2 × 21.2 / 9.8 ≈ 4.33 s

H = 21.2² / (2 × 9.8) ≈ 22.9 m

R = 30² × sin(90°) / 9.8 ≈ 91.8 m

At 45°, the range is maximised at approximately 91.8 m.

Key Insight: All projectile motion problems reduce to two separate 1D kinematics problems — one horizontal (constant velocity) and one vertical (constant acceleration). The link between them is always time t.
