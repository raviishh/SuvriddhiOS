Gas Laws

Basic Properties of Gases

Gases consist of particles in constant, random motion. Unlike solids and liquids, gases expand to fill any container they occupy. The behaviour of a gas can be described using four key variables: pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T), and amount of gas (n).

Ideal Gas: A theoretical gas whose particles have no intermolecular forces and occupy no volume themselves. Real gases approximate ideal behaviour at low pressure and high temperature.

Boyle's Law

Robert Boyle discovered in 1662 that for a fixed amount of gas at constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely proportional.

Boyle's Law:

P V = constant   →   P1 V1 = P2 V2

Where:

  • P = pressure (Pa)
  • V = volume (m³ or L)
  • Temperature and amount of gas are held constant

Intuition for Boyle's Law

Imagine compressing a syringe with your thumb over the opening. As you push the plunger in (reducing V), the air inside pushes back harder (increasing P). The total number of collisions per unit area increases because the same number of particles now occupy a smaller space.

Charles' Law

Jacques Charles found in 1787 that for a fixed amount of gas at constant pressure, volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature.

Charles' Law:

V / T = constant   →   V1 / T1 = V2 / T2

Where:

  • V = volume
  • T = absolute temperature in Kelvin (K = °C + 273.15)
  • Pressure and amount of gas are held constant

Note: Temperature must always be in Kelvin, never Celsius — otherwise the proportionality does not hold.

Gay-Lussac's Law

At constant volume, the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.

Gay-Lussac's Law:

P / T = constant   →   P1 / T1 = P2 / T2

Where:

  • P = pressure
  • T = absolute temperature (K)
  • Volume and amount of gas are held constant

Real-world example: A car tyre at higher temperatures has higher internal pressure. This is why tyre pressure increases after a long drive — the volume of the tyre is roughly constant, so temperature and pressure rise together.

The Ideal Gas Law

Combining Boyle's, Charles', and Gay-Lussac's laws gives the Ideal Gas Law, which relates all four variables simultaneously.

Ideal Gas Law

The product of pressure and volume of a gas equals the number of moles multiplied by the universal gas constant and the absolute temperature.

Ideal Gas Law:

P V = n R T

Where:

  • P = pressure (Pa)
  • V = volume (m³)
  • n = number of moles of gas (mol)
  • R = universal gas constant = 8.314 J / (mol·K)
  • T = absolute temperature (K)

Avogadro's Law: At constant temperature and pressure, equal volumes of all ideal gases contain the same number of molecules. One mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L at STP (0°C, 1 atm).

Example

Example: Gas Compressed in a Cylinder

2 moles of an ideal gas are at 300 K and occupy 0.05 m³. What is the pressure? If the gas is then heated to 600 K at constant volume, what is the new pressure?

Step 1 — Initial Pressure:

P = nRT / V = (2 × 8.314 × 300) / 0.05

P ≈ 99,768 Pa ≈ 1 atm

Step 2 — After Heating (Gay-Lussac):

P2 / T2 = P1 / T1

P2 = 99,768 × (600 / 300) ≈ 199,536 Pa ≈ 2 atm

Doubling the temperature at constant volume doubles the pressure.

Key Insight: The three individual gas laws (Boyle's, Charles', Gay-Lussac's) are all special cases of the Ideal Gas Law where one variable is held constant. In any problem, identify which variable is fixed to select the right relationship.
